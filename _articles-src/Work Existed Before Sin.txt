Work Existed Before Sin

A letter to the person who feels guilty for loving what they do.
You have felt it. That quiet unease sitting between Sunday and Monday morning.
Sunday you sang. You prayed. You felt close to something real. Then Monday arrived with its deadlines and invoices and the particular exhaustion of doing something hard for hours, and somewhere in the transition you began to wonder whether the sacred thing you touched on Sunday had anything to do with the ordinary thing demanding your hands on Monday.
It does. More than you know.
But we have to go back further than you might expect. Further than the fall. Further than the curse. All the way back to before anything went wrong.
Before the Ground Was Hard
Genesis 2:15. Before the serpent. Before the fruit. Before the shame and the hiding and the sound of God walking in the garden while two people wished they were somewhere else. Before all of it, this: The Lord God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it.
Work was not the punishment. Work was the assignment. God did not hand Adam a task because something had broken. He handed him a task because something had been made. The garden was real and it needed tending and God, in His wisdom, decided that the image-bearer He had just fashioned from dust would be the one to tend it.
This is not a small thing. This is the first job description in human history, and it was written before sin entered the story.
The God Who Worked First
You cannot understand human work without first understanding that God works.
Genesis 1 is not primarily a scientific account. It is a portrait of a God who makes things. Who separates and fills and forms and names and steps back and calls it good. Six days of creative, purposeful, productive work. And on the seventh day He rested, not because He was tired, but because the work was complete.
When God made you in His image, He made you in the image of a worker. The impulse you feel to build something, to solve a problem, to write a line of code that finally does what it was supposed to do, to look at something finished and call it good, that is not vanity. That is not worldliness dressed in productivity. That is the image of God in you doing exactly what it was designed to do.
Dorothy Sayers, whose clarity on this subject has never been improved upon, wrote in Creed or Chaos that the church has forgotten, or perhaps never truly grasped, that work is not primarily a thing one does to live, but the thing one lives to do. She argued that work is the full expression of the worker's faculties, the medium in which he offers himself to God. She wrote that in 1949. It has been true since Genesis 2.
What the Curse Actually Said
Here is where we must be careful, because this is where the confusion entered.
Genesis 3:17. After the fall. God speaks to Adam and the ground is cursed. In pain you shall eat of it all the days of your life. Thorns and thistles it shall bring forth for you. The work becomes hard. The resistance increases. The gap between effort and outcome widens in ways that were never part of the original design.
But notice what God did not curse. He cursed the ground. He did not curse the work. The thorns were new. The tending was not.
What sin did to work was not abolish it but burden it. It introduced futility into something that was made for fruitfulness. It made the thing God designed for flourishing feel like punishment, which is exactly what the enemy would want you to conclude. That work is the curse. That the Monday feeling is proof that the sacred and the ordinary cannot coexist.
They were made to coexist. They were never meant to be separated.
The Redemption of Monday
Colossians 3:23 does not say work hard at church activities as though serving the Lord. It says whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord and not for men. Whatever. The code you write at midnight. The invoice you send. The analysis you run until the data finally tells its story. The design you rebuild for the fourth time because it still is not right. Whatever.
Paul is not collapsing the distinction between sacred and secular. He is abolishing it. Under the lordship of Christ, the category of secular work simply does not exist for the believer. There is work done for God and work done for lesser things. The task itself is not the determinant. The posture is.
Martin Luther understood this with a force that the medieval church had entirely lost. He wrote that the milkmaid who milks her cows to the glory of God is doing work as holy as the priest offering mass. The Reformation recovered not just the gospel but the dignity of ordinary labour. It gave Monday back to God.
So Keep Working
You are not less spiritual because you love what you do with your hands and your mind. You are not betraying Sunday by caring deeply about Monday. The same God who met you in worship designed you for work, and He is not absent when the screen turns on and the work begins.
Do it well. Do it honestly. Do it as someone who knows that the first worker in history was God Himself, and that you are, in some small and creaturely way, continuing what He started.
