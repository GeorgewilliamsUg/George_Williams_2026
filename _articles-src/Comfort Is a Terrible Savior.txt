Nobody Is Self-Made
My father-in-law was showing me photographs from a farm I'd never heard him mention, black and white, curling at the corners, a boy about nine standing barefoot beside a plow horse twice his size. He told me the story behind each one slowly, the uncle who taught him to weld, the neighbor who cosigned his first loan when the bank wouldn't, the teacher who paid his exam fees out of her own pocket without ever mentioning it again. I had known this man for eleven years as a self-made success, the phrase he used about himself at every family dinner, the story he told his grandchildren about grit and nobody handing him anything. Sitting there with the photographs in my lap, I realized how much of his own history he had quietly edited out, not from dishonesty exactly, but from the same instinct that makes all of us prefer the version where we did it alone.
I thought about Deuteronomy chapter eight later that week, the warning Moses gives the Israelites just before they enter a land they didn't clear or build, houses they didn't construct, wells they didn't dig, and he tells them plainly to beware lest they say in their heart, my power and the might of my hand have gotten me this wealth. What strikes me is that the warning comes right at the threshold of genuine, hard-won success, after forty years of real walking and real waiting, not before it. The danger Moses names isn't laziness pretending to be labor. It's true labor forgetting the ground it stood on to do the laboring.
I don't fully know how to hold both truths at once, that my father-in-law worked with his hands until they bled and that none of it happened in a vacuum, that effort and grace were never actually in competition. I still feel the pull to tell my own story as though I built it from nothing.
I put the photographs back in their box and thanked him for showing me. That felt like enough for one evening.
