Your Mind Is Under Attack — And You Don't Even See It
The most dangerous occupation is the one where the occupied still believe they are free.
In the high-speed corridors of 2026, the siege is not carried out with iron or fire. It is carried out with convenience. We have built a world that anticipates desire before desire fully forms, smoothing the jagged edges of thought until the mind is as flat and featureless as a river stone.
The Illusion of Choice
We think we are searching. We are being herded.
Every algorithm is a shepherd, and we have become a remarkably compliant flock. We have traded the slow, difficult labor of discernment for the efficiency of a curated reality. G.K. Chesterton once wrote that the madman is not the man who has lost his reason but the man who has lost everything except his reason. Today we are losing even that. A mind that refuses to anchor itself in something objective will drift into whatever current runs strongest. In 2026, that current has been engineered by people who profit from your shallowness.
The Death of Depth
A well-built wall stands because it was laid stone by stone with mortar that took time to cure. Our attention is the opposite. We scroll because we can no longer stay. We have lost the capacity to sit with a difficult passage of Scripture, a complex block of code, or a grieving friend without reaching instinctively for the glow in our pockets.
C.S. Lewis warned in The Screwtape Letters that the devil's goal is not grand temptation but noise. Keep the patient from silence, Screwtape advises, and you keep him from God. You also keep him from himself.
This is the attack: the slow, systematic dismantling of your capacity for depth.
The Resistance
How do you fight a war that turns your own comfort into a weapon? You reintroduce friction.
Build with your hands. Write code from scratch. Fix something broken. Garden. Remind your mind that the physical world has no undo button and that this is a feature, not a fault.
Seek silence. If your life runs on a constant stream of audio and notifications, you are a passenger. Turn it off. Sit in the discomfort until the silence becomes familiar, and then until it becomes nourishing.
Read the ancients. Do not only consume what was published this morning. Read the men who died centuries ago and whose words survived the test of time. Augustine, Owen, Calvin, Lewis. Their sentences demand something of you. That demand is the point.
The Final Tally
You are not a data point to be optimized. You are, as Francis Schaeffer put it, a significant man in a significant world, made by a God who speaks and acts and cares about what you do with your mind.
The world wants your attention because your attention is the only thing you truly own. It is the soil in which your thoughts, your faith, your relationships, and your work either take root or wither.
Do not scatter it for a handful of digital dust.
Stand your ground. Go deep. Build something that lasts.
