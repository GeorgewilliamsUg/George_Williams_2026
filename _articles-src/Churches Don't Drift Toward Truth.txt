Churches Don't Drift Toward Truth
I was on the church budget committee the year we voted to cut the visitation ministry, and I remember sitting in that fluorescent-lit fellowship hall telling myself it was practical, just numbers, just a program that wasn't drawing enough volunteers anymore. Nobody in that room decided to stop caring about the homebound and the grieving. We just decided, one reasonable line item at a time, over several years, to stop funding the structures that made caring for them possible. I drove home that night with an unease I couldn't quite name, the sense that something had shifted underneath us without anyone choosing it.
I think about that meeting whenever I read Hebrews chapter two, where the writer warns that we must pay much closer attention to what we have heard, lest we drift away from it, and the word there is nautical, a ship slipping from its mooring not because a storm hit but because nobody checked the rope. That has unsettled something in me that a more dramatic warning never could. I had always pictured apostasy as a door someone walks through, a decision, a moment you could point to later and say, there, that is where it went wrong. But a drifting ship never has that moment. It only has the slow accumulation of small failures to correct course, each one defensible on its own.
I don't know how to fully guard against that in myself, let alone in a congregation of imperfect people trying to do their best with limited time. I still sit on committees. I still make the practical case sometimes when the sentimental one would cost more than we have. But I've started asking, in rooms like that one, what rope we might be quietly letting go of, and I don't always get an answer I like.
I went back the next month and asked whether we could restore even a small piece of that ministry. It wasn't a fix. It was just paying attention again.
