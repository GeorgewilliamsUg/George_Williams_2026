The Respectable Sin Nobody Talks About

There is a sin so common in the church that we have stopped calling it sin.

We have given it better names. We call it being realistic. We call it having concerns. We call it wisdom, which is perhaps the cruelest rename of all, because wisdom is exactly what it kills.

The sin is worry. And before you nod politely and move on, stay with me a moment, because I am not talking about the ordinary anxiety of a difficult life. I am talking about the chronic, low-grade, theologically revealing habit of living as though God is not actually in control of the thing currently keeping you awake at night.

That is not a personality trait. That is a truth claim. And it is a false one.

The Grading System God Never Endorsed

We have built, without quite meaning to, a hierarchy of respectable and unrespectable sins. At the bottom, the ones that get people removed from leadership, the ones that make the congregation go quiet. At the top, the ones we barely notice because everyone has them, because they wear the clothing of virtue, because they do not embarrass anyone at the Sunday morning handshake.

Worry lives at the top of that list. Comfortably. Unchallenged.

Nobody calls a prayer meeting to confess anxiety. Nobody steps down from ministry because they could not stop catastrophising about the future. Nobody treats it with the seriousness we reserve for the sins that are visible and socially costly.

And yet Jesus, in the Sermon on the Mount, places worry alongside the things that reveal where your treasure actually is. Matthew 6:25 does not say try not to worry too much. It says do not be anxious about your life. The same Jesus who said you shall not murder said do not be anxious. He did not grade one as serious and the other as understandable. He called both of them a failure to live in the reality of who God is.

What Worry Is Actually Saying

Here is the part that should unsettle us.

Worry is not simply an emotion. It is a theological statement. When you lie awake rehearsing the worst possible outcome, when you cannot release the thing your hands cannot control, when the peace that Paul describes in Philippians 4 feels like a description of a country you have never visited, you are not just feeling something. You are believing something.

You are believing that God may not be enough for this particular problem. That His sovereignty, which you affirm on Sunday with great conviction, has an asterisk next to it when applied to your specific situation on Wednesday at 3am.

Tozer, in The Knowledge of the Holy, writes that what comes into our minds when we think about God is the most important thing about us. He is right. And chronic worry is one of the most honest indicators of what we actually think about God when the lights go out and the performance stops and we are alone with the thing we cannot fix.

It says He is not quite big enough. Not quite close enough. Not quite trustworthy enough for this.

The Philippians 4 Prescription

Paul writes Philippians from prison. We must keep saying this because we keep forgetting it. He is not writing from a comfortable study with a cup of tea and resolved circumstances. He is chained. His future is uncertain. The church he loves is fragmented by internal conflict. And from that place he writes what is perhaps the most counter-intuitive instruction in all of his letters.

Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus (Philippians 4:6-7).

Do not be anxious about anything. Not most things. Not the small things. Anything.

The prescription is not positive thinking. It is not the management of perspective. It is prayer, specific and thankful, and then the active transfer of the burden to the One whose shoulders were made for it. Peter says it identically in his first letter: casting all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you (1 Peter 5:7). The word casting is the word you use for throwing something with intention. Not placing gently. Throwing. The decisive, deliberate act of a person who has decided they are done carrying what was never theirs to carry.

The Thing About Peter

Peter is worth staying with for a moment. Because Peter wrote those words about casting anxiety and he wrote them as a man who knew what it was to sink.

Matthew 14. Jesus walks on water. Peter climbs out of the boat and for one extraordinary moment he walks on water too. Then he sees the wind. He sees the waves. He takes his eyes off the One who had called him out, and the moment he does, the water stops holding him.

Jesus does not let him drown. He reaches out immediately. But He also says something: O you of little faith, why did you doubt?

The doubt was not intellectual. It was attentional. Peter stopped looking at Christ and started looking at circumstances, and his theology, which had been sufficient to step out of the boat, collapsed the moment the wind got loud enough.

We do this constantly. We step out in faith and then we look at the waves and we go under. Not because God changed. Because our attention did.

The Respectable Part

Here is why we never deal with this seriously.

Worry looks responsible. It looks like you are taking things seriously, like you are not naively ignoring the real and difficult facts of your situation. In some Christian communities it even masquerades as intercessory burden, as though the weight you are carrying at 2am is actually prayer rather than anxiety wearing a spiritual costume.

John Stott, in his commentary on the Sermon on the Mount, draws the line with characteristic precision. He distinguishes between what he calls responsible planning, which is wise and biblical, and what he calls anxious worry, which is the practical denial of God's fatherhood. He argues that Jesus is not forbidding us to think carefully about the future. He is forbidding us to live as though the future belongs to chance rather than to a Father who knows what we need before we ask.

The difference is not in the action. It is in the spirit underneath the action. Planning says I will do what I can and trust God with what I cannot. Worry says I will do what I can and then lie awake rehearsing all the ways it might not be enough.

One is wisdom. The other is practical atheism with a Christian vocabulary.

The Invitation Underneath the Rebuke

I want to end here because I think it matters.

Jesus does not rebuke anxiety the way a frustrated employer rebukes a failing worker. He rebukes it the way a father rebukes a child who is weeping in the dark over something the father has already handled. There is tenderness underneath the correction. There is an invitation inside the command.

Look at the birds of the air, He says in Matthew 6:26. Consider the lilies of the field (Matthew 6:28). He is not being poetic. He is pointing. He is saying look at what I have already done in the world I made and ask yourself honestly whether the God who feeds sparrows and clothes wildflowers is likely to abandon you in your particular darkness.

The answer the creation gives, if you are still enough to hear it, is no.

He is not going to abandon you. He has not forgotten the thing you cannot stop thinking about. He is not scrambling for a solution. He is sovereign over the very thing that is making you lose sleep, and He has been sovereign over it since before you knew it existed.

You can put it down.

Not because it does not matter. But because it is already in hands far steadier than yours.

And those hands have never once dropped anything they were holding.
