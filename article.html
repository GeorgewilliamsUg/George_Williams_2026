Busy Isn't Faithful

I was standing in the church kitchen at nine on a Tuesday night, laminating name tags for a ministry fair that started in eleven hours, having already led a Bible study, picked up the dry cleaning for the pastor's wife's mother's funeral, and answered forty-some texts about a potluck that wasn't even mine to organize. My husband called to ask when I was coming home and I heard myself say, with something close to pride, that this was just what the season required, that someone had to do it. I laminated three more name tags after I hung up before I let myself notice that nobody had actually asked me to do any of this. I had simply kept saying yes until saying yes became indistinguishable, in my own mind, from being faithful.

Churches Don't Drift Toward Truth

I was on the church budget committee the year we voted to cut the visitation ministry, and I remember sitting in that fluorescent-lit fellowship hall telling myself it was practical, just numbers, just a program that wasn't drawing enough volunteers anymore. Nobody in that room decided to stop caring about the homebound and the grieving. We just decided, one reasonable line item at a time, over several years, to stop funding the structures that made caring for them possible. I drove home that night with an unease I couldn't quite name, the sense that something had shifted underneath us without anyone choosing it.

Six. Six people, after all those emails and all that praying over the sign-up sheet, and I sat at the red light replaying every reason I could have skipped it myself, wondering if anyone would have noticed either way. It wasn't dramatic, this feeling. It was just tired and small and quiet, the kind of discouragement that doesn't announce itself so much as settle into the seat beside you.

Nobody Is Self-Made

My father-in-law was showing me photographs from a farm I'd never heard him mention, black and white, curling at the corners, a boy about nine standing barefoot beside a plow horse twice his size. He told me the story behind each one slowly, the uncle who taught him to weld, the neighbor who cosigned his first loan when the bank wouldn't, the teacher who paid his exam fees out of her own pocket without ever mentioning it again. I had known this man for eleven years as a self-made success, the phrase he used about himself at every family dinner, the story he told his grandchildren about grit and nobody handing him anything. Sitting there with the photographs in my lap, I realized how much of his own history he had quietly edited out, not from dishonesty exactly, but from the same instinct that makes all of us prefer the version where we did it alone.

What Poverty Taught Me About Riches

I was counting coins on my kitchen table again, arranging them into small silver towers to see if they'd stretch to cover both the rent balance and a bag of maize flour, and they wouldn't, not by a wide enough margin to matter. My phone buzzed with a mobile money request from my brother, and I stared at it the way you stare at a wound you can't afford to treat, then set the phone face down and kept stacking coins that had already been counted twice. Somewhere outside a neighbor's radio was playing a gospel song about God's abundant blessings, and I remember feeling something close to irritation at it, the way you can feel irritated at a joke that isn't landing because it's too close to your actual life.
