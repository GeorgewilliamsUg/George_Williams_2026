Nobody Is Self-Made

A box of old photographs sits open on a kitchen table, curling at the corners, black and white, a boy of nine standing barefoot beside a plow horse twice his size. Behind that single image are the ones nobody keeps in albums: an uncle who taught the welding, a neighbor who cosigned a loan the bank refused, a teacher who quietly paid an exam fee and never mentioned it again. And yet the story usually told about that boy, grown now and successful, is the story of grit alone, of nobody handing him anything. It is not a lie exactly. It is simply the version with the ground edited out.

Nobody Is Self-Made

Deuteronomy chapter eight carries a warning that lands at an unexpected moment, not before the Israelites work but after. Moses speaks to a people about to enter a land they did not clear, houses they did not build, wells they did not dig, and tells them to beware lest they say in their heart that their own power and the strength of their own hand have gotten them this wealth. The caution arrives at the threshold of real, hard-won success, after forty years of actual walking and actual waiting. What Moses names is not laziness dressed up as labor. It is labor itself forgetting the ground it stood on while laboring.

Holding both truths at once is not simple. Hands can bleed with real work and still stand on borrowed ground; effort and grace were never actually in competition, though they are so often spoken of as rivals. The pull to narrate a life as something built from nothing is old and persistent, and no amount of correct theology fully removes it.

The photographs go back in their box, unresolved and unrehearsed. Some ground is only visible once someone else points to it.
