Who I Am

I came to the Bible
looking for God.

Not inspiration. Not comfort. Not a framework for better living. I came looking for God — and what I found changed everything.

Every page tells
one story.

I study the Bible book by book, chapter by chapter. Not as a religious habit, but because I am convinced that every page of Scripture is telling one story — the story of a holy God who has been glorifying Himself from before the foundation of the world, pursuing a people for Himself, and doing it all through one man: Jesus Christ.

My convictions have been sharpened by men who took the Word seriously — John Stott, John MacArthur, John Piper, A.W. Pink, A.W. Tozer, C.S. Lewis. But I hold their work loosely. What I hold firmly is the text.

I believe theology must land somewhere. It should produce worship, humility, and fire in your bones. That conviction became Go&Train — an initiative I founded to equip rural pastors who have little access to formal theological training.

I am also a writer, a data analyst, and a web developer working in Python and Django. I carry a dream called HopeAbound — a vision for confronting poverty not just with programs but with presence and truth.

Obedience Is Not Optional — And God Did Not Mean It to Be Easy

George  ·  June 9, 2025  ·  3 min read

We have made following Jesus feel like a suggestion. Like a lifestyle upgrade you opt into when it's convenient.

But Jesus never asked for your opinion on His commands. He gave them — and He expects them to land somewhere real. Not in your head. Not on your Instagram caption. In your life.

The problem is not that we disagree with obedience. Most Christians will nod when you mention it. The problem is that we have quietly separated belief from behavior. We say we trust God, but we trust our own instincts when it costs something to trust His word.

Jesus said it plainly: "Why do you call me 'Lord, Lord,' and do not do what I say?" (Luke 6:46). That question is not rhetorical. It is an indictment. And it lands on all of us who have learned to live very comfortably in the gap between what we say we believe and what we actually do.

Here is what I have come to understand: obedience is not the price you pay for grace. It is the evidence that grace has actually done something in you. A changed heart produces changed behavior. Not perfectly — but genuinely.

And no, it will not always be easy. Jesus never said it would be. He said take up your cross. That is not a metaphor for mild inconvenience. It is a call to die — to the self that insists on its own comfort, its own rights, its own version of how things should go.

The question today is simple: where is obedience costing you something? That's probably exactly where God is working.
